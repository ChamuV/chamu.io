Overview

This project was my Master‚Äôs dissertation at Oxford, supervised by Professor Philip Maini and Ms Rebecca Crossley. I had always wanted to work at the intersection of mathematics and biology, and this project was my first real opportunity to do so. After learning how to solve PDEs numerically and exploring more mathematical biology, this felt like a chance to bring everything together in a larger, research-scale project.

The work explores how a clump of tumour cells interacts with the surrounding network of healthy cells, known as the extracellular matrix (ECM). Our model made the ECM more realistic by allowing it to both regenerate and degrade, giving it a dual role in invasion ‚Äî not just as a barrier, as in most previous models, but also as an active participant that can influence and guide tumour movement. The study examined this model analytically, numerically, and asymptotically in one dimension.

Model Description

The model we used for our study was a coupled system describing the interaction between the tumour cell density \(u(x,t)\) and the extracellular matrix (ECM) density \(m(x,t)\):

\[ \begin{aligned} u_t &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\big(D\,m(1 - m)\,u_x\big) + r\,u(1 - u), \\[4pt] m_t &= \alpha(1 - m) - \lambda\,u\,m. \end{aligned} \]

Here, \(u_t\) and \(m_t\) represent the temporal evolution of the tumour and ECM densities. The diffusion coefficient depends on the ECM through the nonlinear term \(m(1 - m)\), which means tumour cells move most effectively when the ECM is only partially degraded, and not when it is fully intact or absent. The reaction term \(r\,u(1 - u)\) models logistic tumour growth.

The ECM evolves through two opposing processes: a regeneration term \(\alpha(1 - m)\), representing natural recovery, and a degradation term \(\lambda\,u\,m\), describing breakdown by tumour cells. Together, these give the ECM a dual role in the invasion process ‚Äî resisting movement when intact but facilitating it when partially degraded.

The system is considered on a one-dimensional domain with no-flux boundary conditions and initial profiles representing a localised tumour invading a uniform ECM. All variables are nondimensionalised so that \(r\), \(\alpha\), and \(\lambda\) control, respectively, tumour growth, ECM regeneration, and ECM degradation.

To understand the effect of ECM recovery, we consider two regimes:

  • Without regeneration: \(\alpha = 0\) (ECM only degrades).
  • With regeneration: \(\alpha > 0\) (ECM can recover).

Travelling-Wave Analysis

We analyse steady invasion fronts using travelling-wave solutions \(u(x,t)=U(\xi)\) and \(m(x,t)=M(\xi)\), with \(\xi=x-ct\). Using a suitable desingularisation and the auxiliary variable \(P=U'\), the system in the wave frame becomes:

\[ \begin{aligned} U' &= P, \\[3pt] P' &= -\,c\,P - U(1 - U)\,M(1 - M), \\[3pt] M' &= \frac{1}{c}\,M(1 - M)\big(\lambda\,U\,M - \alpha(1 - M)\big). \end{aligned} \]

Numerical Results

We also ran numerical simulations of the model to visualise the invasion dynamics and compare them against the analytical predictions. Whilst several features matched expectations from the travelling-wave and asymptotic analyses, the simulations also revealed distinctive behaviours that are unique to each regime.

No regeneration (\(\alpha = 0\))

Without regeneration, the ECM is progressively eroded behind the tumour front, producing a sharp interface between invaded and uninvaded regions. The front travels at an approximately constant speed and the behaviour resembles a degenerate Fisher‚ÄìKPP wave: diffusion effectively shuts down in the depleted region, the ECM gradient localises near the front, and the tumour density saturates quickly behind it.

With regeneration (\(\alpha > 0\))

Introducing regeneration changes the dynamics qualitatively: the ECM partially recovers behind the front, invasion becomes smoother and broader, and the speed is reduced relative to the purely degradative case. Transient oscillations can appear in \(u\) and \(m\) near the leading edge, reflecting the local competition between degradation and recovery; for larger \(\alpha\), the front settles into a slower, balanced configuration.

Conclusion

We introduced a new model for tumour invasion that captures a more intricate role of the extracellular matrix (ECM), incorporating both its degradative and regenerative behaviour. Through a combination of travelling-wave analysis, asymptotic methods, and numerical simulations, we explored how these opposing mechanisms shape invasion dynamics.

The results revealed both expected and unexpected features: FKPP-like fronts in the absence of regeneration, and smoother, slower invasion patterns once ECM recovery was included. These findings suggest that the interplay between degradation and regeneration is fundamental in regulating tumour spread, while also indicating that further analysis is needed to fully characterise the system.

Possible next steps include extending to two spatial dimensions (already under consideration), adding biological effects such as normal-cell populations or tumour-cell death, and exploring deeper mathematical frameworks (e.g. bifurcation or manifold analysis)‚Äîthough the tractability of these directions remains an open question.
