Overview

This isn‚Äôt really a standalone project but rather a numerical skill I picked up while working on several PDE-based models. I first came across IMEX (Implicit‚ÄìExplicit) schemes during my pattern formation coursework and found them extremely useful for speeding up long simulations without losing stability or accuracy. I now use them regularly as part of my solver framework and plan to keep refining their implementation in future work.

IMEX schemes are time-stepping methods that split a system into stiff and non-stiff components. The stiff terms are handled implicitly to maintain numerical stability, while the non-stiff parts are treated explicitly to save computation. This balance makes IMEX schemes particularly effective for reaction‚Äìdiffusion and nonlinear diffusion problems, where fully explicit methods would require prohibitively small time steps and fully implicit ones would be computationally expensive.

Description

Partial differential equations are inherently difficult to solve, and analytical solutions exist only for a limited class of problems under specific simplifying assumptions. One practical approach to studying such systems is through numerical approximation, where the continuous domain is replaced by a discrete computational grid and the solution is advanced in time using iterative schemes.

We begin by defining the spatial domain $[0,L]$, which is divided into $N$ equal subintervals, giving a step size $\Delta x = L/N$. The corresponding grid points are $x_i = i\Delta x$ for $i = 0, \dots, N$. Although the governing equations may not have a prescribed finite end time, in practice we simulate up to a fixed horizon $T$. Time is discretised into uniform steps of size $\Delta t$, with discrete time levels $t_j = j\Delta t$ for $j = 0, \dots, N_t$. The selection of $\Delta x$ and $\Delta t$ must balance accuracy, stability, and computational cost, and is therefore a key part of the numerical design process.

For systems that contain stiff diffusion terms and nonlinear reaction components, such as reaction‚Äìdiffusion equations, direct numerical integration can be challenging due to the coexistence of processes operating on different timescales. To make the problem tractable, the governing equation is separated into two parts:

$$u_t = \mathbf{L}(u,m) + \mathbf{N}(u),$$

where $\mathbf{L}$ represents the diffusive operator and $\mathbf{N}$ denotes the nonlinear or reactive component. Here, $u$ represents the species of interest. This separation, known as operator splitting, allows each contribution to be handled differently: the diffusion term is typically treated implicitly to enhance numerical stability, while the nonlinear term is treated explicitly to maintain computational efficiency. The resulting semi-discrete formulation provides a flexible structure that can be combined with various time-stepping schemes.

Some of the schemes considered in this study are listed below.
